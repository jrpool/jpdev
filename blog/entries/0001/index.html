Mirror, mirror on the wall, who’d be the fairest mayor of all?

Jonathan Robert Pool

Which New York mayoral candidates know how to be fair?

Talk is cheap

Don’t ask candidates for New York City mayor where they stand on equality, diversity, and inclusion. It’s a softball question. You know they’ll say I’m in favor.

Instead, figure out how competent at fairness the candidates are.

How? Not easy, but doable.

Measuring fairness competence

Every serious candidate has a website. But a competent candidate knows it isn’t enough to say Make me a website, or even Make me a gorgeous website. Competent candidates know that

  • there are gaping digital divides;
  • websites can bridge them only with user-friendly, inclusive, and accessible design and engineering;
  • laws require websites to comply with accessibility standards;
  • thousands of public and private website owners get sued every year for violating those standards;
  • and, nonetheless, New York City government is replete with user-confusing web pages.

Candidates good not only at preaching but also at practicing fairness decide—and know how—to procure clean, easy, and accessible campaign websites, so New Yorkers across the educational, age, and disability spectra can browse, get information, ask questions, and take action.
