Accessibility test validation

Jonathan Robert Pool

If experts fail a test, is the test broken?

Introduction

Digital accessibility is a body of standards and expertise. Accessibility specialists know how to design and implement digital products inclusively. As a result, websites, document files, applications, and devices serve users across a wide range of education, age, ability, equipment, and environments.

To ensure that digital products are accessible, one tests them. But that works only if the tests are valid. So, one must test the tests. How?

Seeking a gold standard

A valid test finds all faults that it encounters and does not find any fault unless it really exists. In other words, it reports neither false positives nor false negatives.

To check for false positives, it would be ideal to have a body of digital products that are known to be completely accessible. Then one could perform a test on those products and expect the test to report no faults. Such a set of perfect products could be considered a gold standard of accessibility.

Constructing products to serve as a gold standard is a reasonable first step, but it is risky. The very same person who constructs a test also constructs the validator of that test. This is a conflict of interest. For more robust validation, one finds a gold standard that others have created.

A natural candidate for such a gold standard is the set of digital products created by accessibility experts to advertise and propagate their knowledge. They know how to make things accessible, so presumably their own artifacts are, indeed, accessible. After all, if you are in the business of accessibility consulting, would you create an inaccessible website to market your services?

Testing a theory of testing a test

Let us put this idea into practice and discover how well it works.

I have created an accessibility test named Autotest-combo10. I have also identified a set of 41 web pages produced by accessibility experts, to serve as a gold standard. Finally, I have run Autotest-combo10 on these 41 web pages and obtained results, in the form of deficit scores. A score of 0 would indicate that a page has satisfied all of the requirements tested by Autotest-combo10. Any score greater than 0 would indicate that the test has found one or more faults in the page.
