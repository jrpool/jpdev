Accessibility guru, heal thy site

Jonathan Robert Pool

Do experts in accessible websites make accessible websites?

Introduction

Within information technology, there is a digital accessibility industry. Its consultants, companies, nonprofits, trade associations, and government agencies know how to make websites, mobile apps, PDF files, email messages, and kiosks accessible—that is, functional for a wide range of users in a wide range of situations, especially atypical ones arising from disabilities.

Almost invariably, these accessibility experts have websites. If you wanted to find an accessible website, where would you look? Obviously, you would examine the website of an accessibility expert. It would be an exemplar of accessibility excellence.

But that conjecture, though reasonable, would be a mistake. Organizations in the accessibility industry can still have websites that are not accessible, according to the results of a battery of tests.

Findings

The a11y03 test battery of Autotest was executed on the home pages of 74 digital accessibility experts. The websites were assembled mainly from lists published by the International Association of Accessibility Professionals and Raghavendra Satish Peri.

a11y03 generated a deficit score for each page. A score of 0 would indicate that a page passed all the tests. Any deficit score greater than 0 indicates test failures.

The tests were conducted in October and November 2021.

The table above shows that none of the 74 pages got a deficit score of zero. Why?

As the above-cited test documentation explains, a11y03 makes no claim to perfection. All tests are fallible, different tests would produce different results, and there are disagreements on exactly what makes a web page accessible.

However, it would be wrong to dismiss these results. a11y03 includes, among other tests, axe-core, Equal Access, and WAVE, three widely used packages of accessibility tests developed by industry leaders. Only 13 of the pages received a zero (perfect) score on axe-core, 1 on WAVE, and none on Equal Access.

According to one veteran of the industry, this discrepancy should be no surprise, because the marketing department controls the website, and the accessibility experts in a company spend their time serving customers, not checking their own company’s website. Perhaps prospective customers cannot recognize the defects and so do not turn away.

Conclusion

Can a competent specialist have an incompetent website? Yes, but the pattern reminds me of George Bernard Shaw’s maxim, He who can, does. He who cannot, teaches. If I were shopping for an accessibility advisor, I would not trust one whose own website gets poor scores on accessibility tests. I might consider such candidates, but, if so, I would demand explanations: Are the reported deficits valid? If not, why not? If so, do they not refute your claim that you know how to make websites accessible?
