Automatically measuring digital accessibility

Jonathan Robert Pool

Impossible, or just problematic?

Introduction

Web accessibility, or more generally digital accessibility, is similar to physical accessibility. Just as buildings can be made friendly to wheelchair users with ramps and wide doors, websites and mobile apps can be made friendly to people who can’t see well or at all, or move their limbs or mice, with barrier-free design and implementation.

There are numerous methods for measuring web accessibility. This report relies on fully automatic methods. By not relying at all on human testing, I am losing some information that human testers could provide, but I am also gaining replicability and improvability. Others can repeat the same tests to check the results and can create new versions of the tests, and additional tests, to make the results more valid.

The following table reports the results of executing a battery of accessibility tests, named a11y03. The tests performed include three packages of tests created by others:

  • Axe, created by Deque (138 tests)
  • Equal Access Accessibility Checker, created by IBM (163 tests)
  • WAVE, created by WebAIM (110 tests)

In addition, there are 16 tests that I created in order to detect accessibility problems that the above packages are not designed to find. Thus, in all, a11y03 conducts 427 tests.

Not only can accessibility be measured by many different tests, but one can aggregate the results of tests in an infinity of ways to yield a total score, if one wants to compare and rank digital products on accessibility. The a11y03 battery applies weights to the results of individual tests and subtracts discounts from some results to compensate for multiple tests discovering the same problems. The weights and discounts reflect the severity ratings incorporated into the three packages, and also express my own opinions about the relative importances of accessibility problems. These weights and discounts can be modified by others to express other opinions.

The engine used for administering this battery of tests is Autotest.

Findings

The tests include three external packages of tests: Axe, IBM Accessibility Checker, and WAVE. In addition, there are thirteen accessibility tests defined in Autotest, chiefly testing aspects of accessibility that the test packages do not cover.

For the main page of each company’s website, Autotest generated a deficit score. A score of 0 indicates that a page has passed all the tests. Any deficit score greater than 0 indicates that at least one test has found at least one fault on the page.

The tests were conducted in October 2021.
