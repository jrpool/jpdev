R&D notes

Accessibility: an ESG lie detector

Jonathan Robert Pool

A social outcome that corporations cannot hide

Introduction

Many investors and investment experts consider a corporation’s environmental, social, and governance (ESG) qualities to be important determinants of the value of its stock, but measuring those qualities is difficult. ESG qualities are numerous, diversely understood, easy to conceal or falsify, and thus expensive to discover. Public statements of ESG commitment are easy to see, but, as Mobius, von Hardenberg, and Konieczny note (p. 162), promises are cheap. So investors try to measure ESG reality, but, as Jennifer Howard-Grenville argues, the qualities that are easiest to measure tend to be inputs, which are poor predictors of outcomes and impacts.

The social component of ESG typically includes diversity and inclusion (D&I). According to auditing firm PwC, The social element of ESG issues (specifically D&I) can be the most difficult for investors to assess and for companies to show continued progress. … There is a divide among stakeholders on … what the term even means.

Despite the differences, D&I efforts usually address a common catalog of classifications, including race, ethnicity, sex, sexual orientation, age, and disability. The United States Department of Labor says that disability is a natural part of diversity.

Systems equally usable by persons with and without disabilities are termed accessible. Accessible buildings are equipped with features such as ramps and wide doors to accommodate wheelchair users. Accessible websites, mobile apps, email messages, and digital files are designed and constructed to avoid barriers that would particularly frustrate older visitors and persons with durable and temporary disabilities. There are detailed standards in both domains: physical and digital. The latter are incorporated into law in about 20 countries. Article 9 of the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities requires states to promote digital accessibility. In the United States, thousands of federal lawsuits are filed annually claiming digital disability discrimination due to accessibility defects.

Even if most social ESG outcomes are hard to observe, website accessibility is not. Visiting a corporation’s website does not even require travel to a company building. Point your web browser to the website, and there it is.

Website accessibility is also likely to predict other ESG outcomes and impacts. In practice, accessible websites do not just happen; they result from corporate decisions and effective governance. If an enterprise decides to prioritize social equity, inclusion, diversity, software quality, and risk mitigation, and it organizes, manages, staffs, and incentivizes itself to make those priorities real, then it succeeds in making and keeping its digital assets accessible. If it can do that, it can more easily achieve other ESG goals, too.

The accessibility of a website can be measured. There is substantial (even if incomplete) consensus on what makes a website accessible or inaccessible. The cost of measuring the accessibility of a single web page or transaction can range from pennies to hundreds of dollars, depending on the amount of human testing in the measurement process. More human effort tends to improve measurement quality. Fully automated measurement, however, is less costly and more auditable.

Findings

In November 2021 I used a fully automated procedure to measure the accessibility of the home pages of the websites of the 200 corporations that are in the portfolios of four ESG-oriented mutual funds. The funds are:

  • AMDWX: Amana Developing World Fund
  • CCVAX: Calvert Small-Cap Fund
  • NALFX: New Alternatives Fund
  • VEIGX: Vanguard Global ESG Select Stock Fund

The measurement procedure is a11y04, performed by Autotest.

For each page, 427 individual tests were performed. Of these, 411 belonged to three test packages created by others (Deque Axe-core, IBM Equal Access, and WebAIM WAVE). I created the other 16 tests to capture additional accessibility properties. The results were aggregated into a deficit score. A score of 0 would indicate that a page has passed all the tests. Any deficit score greater than 0 indicates test failures.

Conclusion

This table provides evidence that one can find corporations with a wide range of website accessibilities within the portfolios of ESG-oriented mutual funds. None of these funds, evidently, applies a negative accessibility screen to rule out corporations that do not try, or try and fail, to make their websites accessible.

Such evidence is, of course, rebuttable. As the above-cited documentation explains, a11y04 makes no claim to perfection. All tests are fallible, and different tests would produce different results. This page, for example, gets a score of 111 on the same procedure, for reasons that, on review, do not raise accessibility concerns.

Even though all tests can err, disregarding test results is reckless. Some of the test packages used in a11y04 are freely available and used by litigators to identify potential defendants in accessibility complaints. Of these 200 home pages, only one got a perfect 0 score on Axe-core and none got a 0 score on either Equal Access or WAVE. Running those test packages on one’s own website and either correcting or rebutting the reported defects would be prudent risk management.

Investors can easily run Axe-core, WAVE, and other accessibility testing packages in their web browsers. With more effort, they can install, run, and extend open-source testing software, such as Autotest. They do not need to request or compel accessibility disclosures. Web accessibility is self-disclosing.

Activist investors can do more than measure. Shareholder interventions for ESG typically urge management to report. For digital accessibility, however, activists can publish their own reports and urge management to make specific improvements. Each time a firm claims it has done so, one can check the claim by rerunning the tests.

Website accessibility is only one piece of ESG, but measuring it can make an outsize contribution to real-time ESG accountability.
