Charitable accessibility

Jonathan Robert Pool

Which charities make themselves accessible on the web?

Introduction

Making one’s website accessible for all, including persons who face obstacles in browsing the web, is a charitable thing to do. Therefore, it would be reasonable to expect charities to do that.

But that expectation would be wrong. In fact, if you assess the accessibility of the websites of a few dozen charities, you will find a wide range of results.

Findings

I tested the home pages of a collection of mainly USA-based charitable foundations. I selected the charities from those working to advance various causes, chiefly justice, inclusiveness, civil liberties, ecological recovery, waste reduction, population sustainability, nutrition, disaster resiliance, peace, public safety, secularism, evidence-based policy, and knowledge access. I skewed the distribution of local charities somewhat toward New York City.

To perform the tests, I used two slightly different versions (4 and 5) of a procedure named a11y in the Autotest package. The procedure leverages over 400 tests in three widely used packages (Axe, Equal Access, and WAVE) and extends them with 16 custom tests of my own.

For the main page of each organization’s website, the procedure generated a deficit score. A score of 0 would indicate that a page has passed all the tests. Any deficit score greater than 0 indicates test failures.

I performed the tests in October and November 2021.

Conclusion

Although these charities are not representative of the charitable universe in the United States, many of them have missions that would make them sympathetic with accommodations for diversity and inclusion.

Web accessibility mainly aims to ensure that people with various disabilities can successfully use web resources. But, among these charities’ home pages, those of disability-oriented organizations got the lowest (Disability Rights Commission) and the highest (Special Olympics) deficit scores.

All of the websites were found lacking some aspects of accessibility. In this respect, they are similar to international human rights organizations, whose home pages received deficit scores ranging from about 450 to more than 3500.

Measures of accessibility vary, and there is no consensus on the best or the correct way to measure it. Other procedures would not deliver identical results. That is why the a11y procedure runs many tests from several sources. Being fully automated, this procedure can be replicated and double-checked. Being open-source, the details behind the scores that it generates can be inspected and challenged.

More importantly, these organizations can use such results to diagnose and dismantle website barriers and thereby become more inclusive.
