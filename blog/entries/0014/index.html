R&D notes

Verifying the Disability Equality Index

Jonathan Robert Pool

Accessibility scores for 272 web pages of DEI winners

Summary

A new analysis reveals wide variation in conformity to web-accessibility standards by organizations that have won high scores on the Disability Equality Index (DEI). That is not a surprise, since the DEI is based on self-reporting by organizations, not measured performance.

Introduction

The Disability Equality Index (DEI) is a measure of disability inclusion and equality in organizations in the United States with 500 or more employees.

Organizations that choose to participate in the DEI complete a questionnaire and, on the basis of their answers, receive scores. If their scores are 80% or more, their scores are published and they are considered DEI winners.

Some of the questions in the questionnaire deal with digital accessibility: the design and implementation of websites and other digital content to avoid barriers to use. Persons with major and minor disabilities often depend on accessible content. It also benefits users in challenging environments, such as poor lighting, small screens, or moving vehicles.

Since digital accessibility contributes to an organization’s DEI score, we might expect DEI winners to have achieved high-quality digital accessibility.

Findings

In August 2022 I used the software packages Testaro and Testilo to test the home pages of all 272 of the 2021 DEI winners.

The 1230 tests performed on those pages were created by Deque, IBM, Utah State University, Level Access, Siteimprove, Squiz Labs, Tenon, and individual contributors. Human testing was not performed, so the tests could not detect all accessibility problems, but thousands of problems were identified.

Comments

Not a single home page was found that could not be made more accessible. For example, the page with the best accessibility score, belonging to Fifth Third Bank, contains several embedded web pages (in iframe elements). Each such embedded page needs to declare what language the content it is written in, so blind or other users who are listening to the page by means of a screen reader will hear the correct pronunciation. But these embedded pages do not declare their language.

The DEI score is based on more than digital accessibility. Digital accessibility is covered by questions 3 through 7 of the 7 weighted questions in the Enterprise-wide access section of the questionnaire, and that section contributes 10 points toward the total of 100 points. For example, question 4c asks the organization, please estimate the percentage of your entire externally facing digital products that are accessible.

Thus, there are two reasons to question whether DEI scores and accessibility scores will be highly correlated:

  • Digital accessibility constitutes only about 7 points out of the 100-point DEI score.
  • The DEI score is based on self-reports, not measured performance.

However, if DEI scores are not good predictors of digital accessibility, we have reason to question whether organizations should reap reputational benefits from their DEI scores. Inaccessible websites create substantial risk of litigation, loss of reputation, and loss of market share.

As you review the table, you will notice a low correlation. DEI scores of 80% are not concentrated at the bottom of the table, where one might expect. DEI scores of 100% are not concentrated at the top of the table, where one might expect.

A quick statistic supports this observation. The median accessibility scores are:

  • 2688 among 80% DEI winners
  • 1567 among the 90% DEI winners
  • 2242 among the 100% DEI winners

Thus, the best median accessibility scores were achieved by the middle (90%) group of DEI winners, not the top (100%) group.

The range of accessibility scores was large enough to make proportionally sized bars impractical. So, the bars in the table are sized proportionally to the square roots of the accessibility scores as fractions of the largest accessibility score. So, if one bar is 2 times as long as another bar, the first score is 4 times as large (i.e. bad) as the other score.

Automated tests like these are not perfect and not complete, but they can discover and diagnose many accessibility defects at trivial cost.

Conclusions

This analysis discovered that many DEI winners publish web content with accessibility defects.

DEI scores are not accurate predictors of digital accessibility.

DEI scores are based on what organizations claim. If they claim digital accessibility, they earn DEI points. But it is feasible to subject their websites to accessibility testing, instead of believing their claims.
