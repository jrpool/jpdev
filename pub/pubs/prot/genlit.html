Notes on the Literature of Genocide

Analytical Categories

Here are five basic groups of questions about genocide.

1. Definitions (What is genocide?)
What does "genocide" mean? Is genocide defined by intent, method, or result? Are there scientific, legal, and political definitions, and, if so, are they compatible? Is genocide a special case of a more general category, and are there further special cases of genocide? Can there be degrees of genocide? Is the definition of genocide consensual or conflictual? If there are conflicts over the definition, what are the stakes of the outcome? Is genocide a universal concept or one whose meaning (like "poverty") is contextually variable? Does defining "genocide" have legal or other behavioral consequences?
2. Causes (Why genocide?)
What are the causes and motives of genocide? Under what physical, social, intellectual, and emotional conditions does genocide take place? What makes people lead genocidal movements? What makes people participate in them? What makes people vulnerable to them? Are there innate propensities for or against genocide? Are the motivations for genocide economic, political, religious, or ethnic? Do people participate in genocide out of hatred, fear, cruelty, or impassivity? Do people participate in genocide willingly, neutrally, or reluctantly? Do people make genocidal choices as rational means to ends or as emotional expressions? Do repetitive participants in genocide become less resistant to participation with increasing experience? Do participants in genocide exhibit a need to justify their behavior morally? Are goals of genocidal behavior overtly expressed, and, if so, are they formulated so as to make it possible to characterize a genocide as successful or unsuccessful?
3. Mechanisms (How does genocide work?)
Are there typical life (or death) cycles of genocides? Do they sometimes get aborted early, and, if so, how? What are the leading indicators of genocide? What stages do genocides proceed through? Who starts genocides? Are genocides provoked or encouraged by foreign agents? What messages are disseminated, and how and to whom, by those who organize or direct genocides? How centrally or diffusely are genocides planned and conducted? How do near and distant individuals and organizations respond to evolving genocides? Do the methods used in the performance of each genocide become more efficacious or routine as the genocide ages? Under what conditions do the victims of genocides resist, seek external intervention, seek to flee, panic, become paralyzed, hide, become individually coopted, become double agents, commit suicide, and cooperate meekly? What kinds of nonvictim resistance occur? Do genocides evolve into businesses in which some interests are served by the labor of unkilled victims or in the production or commercialization of corpses? After genocides end, what processes of punishment, retribution, retaliation, reconciliation, disclosure, denial, suppression and falsification of records, and commemoration take place?
4. Effects (What does genocide do?)
What does genocide do to the countries where it takes place? How does it affect politics, society, economics, science, literature, the arts, ethics, and religion? What does it do to the rest of the world? Does genocide matter to those far away? What effects do migrations of genocidal refugees have on countries of refuge and settlement? How does the recurrence of genocide affect movements for the adoption and enforcement of international law? Do individuals and organizations learn lessons from genocides? Do genocides provide opportunities or excuses for transnational collaboration? Do genocidal histories interfere with diplomatic and popular relations between countries related to the perpetrator and victim groups?
5. Solutions (What shall we do about genocide?)
Can the conditions conducive to genocide be eliminated? Can people be educated into genocide-proofness? Would democratization prevent genocide, or guarantee its perpetuation? Is the prevention of genocide a right, or a duty, and, if so, whose? Does genocide invalidate claims to sovereignty? Does the prospect of punishment for genocide deter its commission? Do sanctions against genocidal governments cause genocides to stop? What formal regional and world institutions of intervention against genocide exist, and are they effective? Could genocide be criminalized? What properties would an effective genocide intervention force have? Does ethnic segregation guard against genocide or make it more likely to occur? Is genocide preventable only if committed by small, non-nuclear powers? Can mass publics be mobilized against distant genocides? Do innovations in information technology, telecommunications, and pathology provide new tools for genocide disclosure and control?

effects solutionsPeter BalakianThe Burning Tigris: The Armenian Genocide and America's Response2004Harper PerennialArgument: U.S. policy is evasive, sluggish, resistant to action ... in response to genocide because policy is driven by interests rather than by a moral code. Thus we can see how in the case of Armenia, where there was broad media coverage and massive public empathy, there was no swift govt response. Still there were individuals (in US govt, diplomatic corps, etc.) who did in fact intervene--but as individuals. Misc: It is important to know that the Armenian Genocide prompted two historic responses in the evolution of international ethics. In May 1915, in the midst of World War I, the Allies meeting in London conceived of what they termed "crimes against humanity," in warning the Ottoman government that massacring the Armenian population would violate a fundamental standard of humanity and would have consequences. In 1944, the Polish jurist Raphael Lemkin coined the term genocide and based his definition in large part by what had happened to the Armenians in 1915 and what was happening to the Jews of Europe. By the early 1920s, the American response to the "starving Armenians" was divided between a passionate popular appeal for aid and justice, and the limits of the federal government--the State Department, the White House, and a powerful segment of the Senate, which was isolationist and Republican. The post-World-War-I power alliance with Atatürk's new Turkish Republic and the American drive for oil in the Middle East led to the abandonment of Armenia. In some sense, this paradox would haunt the United States through the 20th century and beyond, when it was faced with the challenge of genocide and other human rights atrocities, whether committed in Europe against the Jews, or in Cambodia, Rwanda, Iraq, or Bosnia. definitions causes effectsWard ChurchillKill the Indian, Save the Man2004City LightsHistorical study. Argument: Indian residential schools (in US and Canada) were a tool of cultural genocide. Advocates a broad definition of genocide. Misc: This concise discussion is a continuum to Churchill's longer 1997 work, A Little Matter of Genocide: Holocaust and Denial in the Americas. The author first analyzes the term genocide (coined by Raphael Lemkin in 1944), stressing that the phrase covers not only physical extermination of a people but also biological genocide (policies to prevent births within a group) and cultural genocide. The Indian residential schools in both the U.S and Canada fall squarely into this latter category, which includes the forced exile of children and the prohibition of the use of a national language or religion. Churchill next turns his attention to the schools themselves, uncovering a host of grim details. In Canada, half of the children sent to residential schools did not survive because of rampant disease, near-starvation diets, and brutal labor. In fact, Churchill observes that survivors display a level of dysfunction similar to that exhibited by concentration camp survivors. Churchill presents a bleak yet utterly necessary history of a brutal system that was in effect until 1990.
